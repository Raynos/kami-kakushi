# Story Bible §0 — the kernel

> Part of [the story bible](README.md). Human-locked canon.

Seven points — the only fixed ground of the story. Everything else —
cast, arcs, tiers, premise framing — earns its place under these.
(Redline history: the reboot plan §4.)

1. **The lie of the vanishing.** *Kami-kakushi* — the spirit-abduction the
   title promises — is a lie the world tells itself to bear what it cannot
   face. Every vanishing has a mundane, worldly cause: debt, flight, shame,
   violence, cold arithmetic — or a plain accident the story never dresses
   up (a landslide, an undertow, a child who simply ran). *(Softened from
   "human cause" by the human, 2026-07-07: a vanishing may be nobody's
   sin, and its truth may be warm — the MC's own is.)* The folk-belief is
   never mocked — believing is grief-work, and the game treats believers
   with kindness — but the truth, when the player finally holds it, is
   always mundane; it may cut deeper than the myth would have, or it may
   hand someone back alive — it is not required to wound. The game permits
   itself at most ONE moment of genuine ambiguity, ever. No supernatural
   cause is ever real, no yokai ever spawns, and the player is never
   teased with magic the fiction won't cash.

2. **Nobody twice unnamed.** A person may enter the story nameless — a
   stranger washed up at the gate, a vanished child in a decade-old rumor,
   a face in a field. But no one the story touches twice stays that way.
   The MC arrives with no name, and the game is the earning of one. The
   vanished are recovered BY NAME — to solve a vanishing is to give
   someone back their name, and with it their story. And once the player
   has learned a name, the fiction never takes it back: nobody we've met
   is ever again "a farmhand," "a pedlar," a prop. Namelessness is where
   every thread starts; leaving someone unnamed twice is the one cruelty
   the game never commits.

3. **Worth written by work.** No one in this world is owed anything by
   blood, destiny, or gift. Every advantage on screen was bought, lucked
   into, or trained — never innate; talent is a story the lucky tell. The
   MC is mediocre by design: what raises him is labor, repeated, until it
   is recorded. And "written" is literal — this is a world of ledgers,
   deed-books, and rank-charts, where worth exists because someone wrote
   it down. Work becomes deeds, deeds become standing, standing becomes a
   name (#2). The game never grants what the player hasn't ground; praise
   is scarce and earned — the fiction never hands out warmth the work
   hasn't paid for, though it needn't produce the ledger every time.

4. **House before the man.** The unit of this story is not the hero but
   the household. The MC's rise is real only as the House's rise — his
   ladder is its ledger (#3), and what ascends from circle to circle is
   the House's standing in the world, with him inside it. Every named
   member serves the House first and their own heart second, and the gap
   between those two is where the drama lives: trust extended because the
   House needs hands, coldness kept because the House cannot afford
   another wound, praise withheld because the House's dignity is spending
   it. The enemy attacks the House, not the man — debt, disrepair, a name
   in decline. The House outlasts everyone in it: choices weigh what they
   cost the House after the chooser is gone. The late game may ask whether
   the man outgrows the House — the asking is drama, and permitted — but
   the answer honors the House.

5. **Partial justice.** The world's wrongs are answered the way real
   wrongs are: incompletely. Some who deserve ruin answer for it; some
   walk; some are never even reached — glimpsed once, protected by
   distance, rank, or paper. Justice, where it lands, is mediated: it
   travels up through other hands, costs its seekers dearly — sometimes
   everything — and arrives smaller than the wrong. The MC's reach has a
   ceiling and the story honors it: no throne room, no audience with the
   untouchable, no final scene where power kneels for the camera. What the
   game promises instead is the other half of the deal: the TRUTH is
   recovered in full even where the justice isn't. Full truth, partial
   justice — that is the contract with the player, and it is never broken
   in either direction.

6. **Form is the fiction.** The game's form — its panels, logs, charts,
   numbers, buttons — is not a window onto the world; it is made OF the
   world. Every surface the player touches is a thing that exists in the
   fiction: the log a house journal, the bestiary a field-guide someone
   actually keeps, the rank chart a banzuke, the balance a ledger in real
   koku and mon. No abstract game-word — XP, buff, level, quest — ever
   reaches the player; every mechanic wears its period name and earns its
   period shape. Surfaces appear because the story produced them: the
   reveal follows the beat, the beat dramatizes the need, and nobody in
   the fiction ever describes the interface (that is the ventriloquism
   this point forbids). When a mechanic and the fiction disagree, exhaust
   redesign before shipping the disagreement.
   *Application:* a new panel or capability is
   dramatized as a NEED the story produced — Kihei sick of burying men
   who can't tell a mamushi from a rat-snake IS the bestiary. No
   grant-list paragraphs, ever.

7. **The world is nobody's mirror.** The world does not exist to assess
   the MC — and it assesses him wrong as often as life does. People
   misread him, suspect him, use him, forget him; some warm slowly, some
   never; the village keeps living when he isn't looking, and he hears of
   it late. Being misread is not an injustice the game corrects — it is
   the weather. The player's true standing lives in the ledgers (#3);
   hearts are slower, stranger, and never owed. A stranger who works hard
   is still a stranger.
   *Application:* distrust, suspicion, and social
   cost are DRAMATIZED as obstacles the player feels — never merely
   narrated ("we'll see if you mean it" is telling, not friction).
