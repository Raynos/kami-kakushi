## 1 · What "koku" actually was

The headline finding: around 1780 Japan ran **two parallel value systems** that a
game must not silently collapse into one — a **rice/assessment** system and a
**cash** system — with conversion friction between them.

- **[FACT] Koku was fundamentally a unit of rice VOLUME**, ~180 litres (10 to,
  ~150 kg), nominally the rice to feed one adult for a year — not intrinsically a
  coin. Formally fixed at 180.39 L in 1891, but the convention is old.
  (Wikipedia *Koku*/*Kokudaka*; Nippon.com; Sumitomo.)
  - **Nuance [INTERP]:** an Edo merchant house-code implies ~**1.8 koku** was the
    *real* annual need per adult male — so "1 koku feeds one person" was an
    idealised accounting convention, not a subsistence measurement.

- **[FACT] Kokudaka was an administrative ASSESSMENT of a domain's total
  productive output** — non-rice crops, seafood, even industrial goods converted
  to a rice-equivalent (rice-less Ezo was still "measured in koku"). It **ranked
  lords** (a lord counted as *daimyō* only at **≥10,000 koku**; Kaga han ≈1.025M
  koku) and set court precedence. An accounting/status unit, **not** a pile of
  actual rice and **not** a coin. (Harvard "Constraining the Samurai" citing
  Smith 1958 / Jansen 1995 / White 1995; Wikipedia; Nippon.com.)

- **[FACT] The assessment diverged sharply from reality.** Actual yield was
  *frequently ~double* the stale official kokudaka a domain's samurai were
  credited with (Jansen 1995), while actual **revenue collected averaged only
  ~40%** of theoretical kokudaka (Wikipedia *Kokudaka*). The *omote-daka*
  (official) vs *uchi-daka* (real) gap is standard historiography. → koku
  **overstated** real spendable income.

So koku was, simultaneously: a **rice measure**, a **domain/stipend assessment**,
a **status rank**, and a **claim on future rice** — never a spendable coin.

## 2 · How people actually transacted — the cash economy

- **[FACT] A real tri-metallic CASH economy** ran alongside rice accounting, and
  was **~180 years old by 1780** (established by Ieyasu, 1601): **gold**
  (ryō/bu/shu, by denomination), **silver** (monme, **by weight**), **copper**
  (mon, **by count**). (Bank of Japan Currency Museum — *primary*; Wikipedia
  *Tokugawa coinage*.)
- **[FACT] Only the gold ladder was truly fixed**: 1 ryō = 4 bu = 16 shu =
  ~4,000 mon. The **inter-metal rates FLOATED** on near-daily market rates set by
  moneychangers (ryōgae): official silver ~**50 → 60 monme per ryō** after the
  Genroku recoinage; copper devalued further later (~6,500 mon/ryō by the 1830s).
  → **cash itself was not one stable currency either.**
- **[FACT] Samurai (incl. daimyō and hatamoto) were paid in RICE stipends
  measured in koku**, which is *exactly why* rice→cash conversion institutions
  were essential. Lords shipped surplus tax-rice to markets — **Osaka's Dōjima
  rice exchange** (authorised 1730) — to liquidate it into currency.
  - **[FACT, refined]** Lower samurai / ashigaru were often paid in **hyō (bales)**
    or **cash rations (fuchi)**; city-level rice→cash conversion was near
    universal. (A stronger claim — that *only ~a dozen* bannermen got cash — was
    **REFUTED** 1-2; cash rations were common.)
- **[FACT] Fudasashi rice-brokers grew wealthy chiefly through MONEY-LENDING**
  (interest) to cash-strapped samurai, not mainly brokerage fees. The mechanism:
  a **"rice-price scissors"** — fixed grain stipends lost purchasing power as the
  economy monetised (a 1730 glut cut grain prices ~45%), pushing samurai into
  debt. (Yamamura, *JEH* 1971; Wikipedia *Rice broker*.) The "textiles and salt"
  examples are paraphrase, not verbatim; revisionists qualify the *magnitude* of
  samurai poverty but not the mechanism.

## 3 · Taxation & domain finance

- **[FACT] Land tax (nengu) was assessed on koku and paid mainly in RICE**, then
  partly sold for cash. Rates **varied enormously (~15%–70%)** across the
  **~260–300 semi-autonomous domains** because **each daimyō set his own rate**.
  The famous "four-public-six-private" (~40%) is the well-known average.
  - **[FACT] Peasant resistance measurably lowered rates:** domains with a
    history of insurrection/flight ran nengu ~5% lower — rebellion and
    foot-voting were real fiscal constraints on the lord.
  - **Caveat:** the 15–70% span is from 1868 records; by ~1780 the **fixed-rate
    (jōmen)** system assessed tax on kokudaka rather than each year's literal
    yield — a lord's income was somewhat *decoupled* from the actual harvest.

- **[INTERP] Domain finance was chronically strained.** Fixed rice income vs a
  monetising cash economy, plus **sankin-kōtai** (alternate-attendance) travel and
  dual-residence costs, is the standard explanation for daimyō debt — but the
  *specifics* of sankin-kōtai as a fiscal drain were **not verified** here (open
  question).

## 4 · Society, village, and the looming shock (thinly sourced — treat as sketch)

The verify pass did **not** substantiate most of the social/political brief.
Recorded here as **[INTERP]** leads to research next, not as fact:

- **[INTERP] Status ≠ wealth, and the "four-class" chart is contested.** The
  shi-nō-kō-shō order was a legal/hereditary status system, not a wealth ranking;
  post-1995 scholarship treats the rigid four-tier pyramid as a Confucian ideal
  repeated as if it were law, and removed it from Japanese textbooks. (Britannica;
  a myth-busting blog — weak source.) A **paper-rich/cash-poor samurai** and a
  **rich-but-low-status merchant** coexisting is the dramatically useful truth.
- **[INTERP] Village self-governance via collective liability.** The
  **gonin-gumi** (five-household groups) bound neighbours to mutual responsibility
  for tax (nengu), crime, and conduct, under a **nanushi/shōya** headman who
  administered the *mura* and collected tax. Strong mechanic candidates (mutual
  surveillance, collective tax liability, mutual aid) — but the claims here are
  single-source and under-verified.
- **[INTERP] The Tenmei famine (began 1782)** is confirmed only as a *date* — a
  natural "looming shock" for a 1780-anchored campaign. Its actual dynamics
  (price spikes, mortality, relief politics, hoarding riots, domain-vs-bakufu
  relief) are **unresearched**.
- **[unverified / brief-only]** giri/on/reputation, patronage and factional
  politics, eta/hinin outcast status — named in the brief, **no verified claims**.

## 5 · Design-oriented synthesis

### The clean 4-resource model the facts point to

The friction between rice-measure / status-rank / tax-claim / cash / credit **is**
the gameplay. A legible model that keeps the friction without the metrology:

- **RICE** — income; taxable; storable; price-volatile; the thing you actually
  grow/collect. Perishes/spoils enough to matter.
- **CASH** (ryō/mon) — spendable, borrowable; you get it by *converting* rice at a
  fluctuating rate.
- **KOKU-RANK** (assessed status) — gates access, obligations, and tiers; **does
  NOT deplete when spent**. This is the "paper wealth" number.
- **DEBT / CREDIT** (the fudasashi lever) — leverage now against future rice, with
  an interest trap.

### The five questions

1. **What players naturally expect "koku" to mean.** A gold-like **spendable
   currency** — one number that goes up and that you spend. (This expectation is
   the trap.)

2. **What's misleading about that.** Koku was a rice *volume* / *assessment* /
   *status rank* / *claim on future rice* — never a coin. Spending "koku" directly
   erases the two things that make the setting distinctive: the **rice→cash→credit
   conversion** and koku's meaning as a **daimyō-ranking status unit**. It also
   hides that koku **overstated** real income (the 40%/2× gap).

3. **Distinctions worth PRESERVING because they create better gameplay.**
   - **Koku-as-rank** — the 10,000-koku daimyō threshold as a clean legibility
     gate between status tiers.
   - **Assessment-vs-reality gap** — the "paper-rich, cash-poor household"
     fantasy; your on-paper standing outruns your purse.
   - **Rice↔cash conversion at a fluctuating rice price** (Dōjima) — a recurring
     *when do I sell?* decision.
   - **The fudasashi debt spiral** — fixed stipend vs rising prices → borrow →
     interest trap; a genuine long-term tension, not a fail-state.
   - **Nengu as a lever with a feedback constraint** — raise the rate for income,
     risk rebellion/flight that *permanently* lowers your ceiling.
   - **Famine (Tenmei 1782) as a looming shock** rather than flavour.
   - **Status ≠ wealth** — the merchant/samurai inversion as a social engine.

4. **What to ABSTRACT or ignore for legibility & fun.**
   - Exact litre/kg conversions of koku.
   - The silver-by-weight vs gold-by-count vs copper-by-count metrology — collapse
     to a simple **ryō/mon cash** resource.
   - Near-daily exchange-rate floats — model as **periodic price swings**, not a
     live forex sim.
   - The full 260–300-domain map — the player's province plus a few neighbours is
     enough.

5. **Open questions to drive the next design phase.**
   - **Village mechanics:** gonin-gumi collective liability + nanushi/shōya roles
     need dedicated sourcing before the village tier is designed.
   - **Social/honour layer:** shi-nō-kō-shō, giri/on/reputation, eta/hinin,
     patronage/faction — the *engine of the rise fantasy* — is essentially
     unresearched. Highest-value next pass.
   - **Sankin-kōtai & domain debt:** the natural late-game money-sink and
     political-risk lever — verify before modelling the regional scale.
   - **Tenmei famine dynamics:** price spikes, mortality, relief politics — needed
     to make it the central shock rather than a tooltip date.
   - **Numbers are directional, not balance-ready:** the 15–70% span is 1868; the
     silver rate is period-specific (~50 early → ~60 post-Genroku); re-pin every
     figure to ~1780 before hard-coding.

## Refuted / excluded claims (kept for the record)

- **REFUTED (1-2):** "only ~a dozen bannermen received cash stipends" — cash
  rations (fuchi) and city rice→cash conversion were common, not a rare exception.
- **REFUTED (0-3):** "a daimyō's rank ranged from 3,000 to 100,000 koku" — the
  10,000-koku floor means a 3,000-koku holder is a **hatamoto**, not a daimyō.
