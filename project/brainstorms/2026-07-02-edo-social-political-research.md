## 1 · Status was ideological ordering over a real, enforced divide

- **[FACT] The shi-nō-kō-shō four-class *ranking* was a Confucian ideal, not
  enforced law** — post-1995 scholarship (Yoshida/Tsukada) reclassifies
  peasant/artisan/merchant as *equal-standing* categories; the ranked chart was
  removed from Japanese textbooks (confirmed by the publisher). **But the
  samurai / commoner / outcaste (mibun) divide WAS genuinely enforced** — sword and
  surname marked it. So status was neither pure fiction nor a clean 4-tier pyramid.
- **[FACT] Mobility was permeable at the LOW end, sealed at the top.** Peasant ↔
  townsman ↔ low-samurai movement was real; middle-and-high samurai was nearly
  closed. A talent could rise only a *few steps* beyond birth (Ray Moore, *JAS*
  1970). → **[DESIGN] The game's own progression should feel this asymmetry:** the
  early rungs (labourer → service → village) are permeable and fast; the
  samurai-elite ceiling is *hard*, earned, and rare.
- **[FACT] Status was worn as concrete tokens:** surname (myōji), the two swords
  (taitō/daishō), and the (heavily-conditioned) right to cut down a commoner
  (kirisute-gomen) — grantable to exceptional commoners (e.g. powerful headmen). →
  **[DESIGN] legible, ownable status TOKENS** that make a rise *tangible* — you can
  *see* you've risen (you now bear a name / the swords), not just a bigger number.
- **[FACT] gōshi (country samurai)** are the defined **middle rung** a riser
  targets — below castle-town samurai (hanshi), above peasants, keeping
  surname + swords. → **[DESIGN] the concrete T2–T3 milestone rank.**

## 2 · How a labourer could *actually* rise (the crux)

- **[FACT] Patronage / personal favour was the true engine.** **Tanuma Okitsugu** —
  son of a Kishū *ashigaru* — rose through successive shoguns' trust from a
  600-koku retainer to a **57,000-koku daimyō and rōjū** (senior councillor), peak
  power ~1770s–1786. **The single best real-world exemplar of this game's whole
  fantasy: low-origin service → favour → national power.** Caveat: a *two-
  generation* arc, and contemporaries called it "unusual" — a template, **not a
  norm.** → **[DESIGN] the spine of the campaign; favour/patronage is the lever,
  and it should feel exceptional.**
- **[FACT] Bought status existed but was stigmatised.** Wealthy merchants/farmers
  could buy gōshi/low-samurai rank (documented prices: ~200 ryō a surname, 500 a
  sword, 1,000 both; 250 for a Sendai samurai register). Kumamoto institutionalised
  it ("Sunshi Gokenin"); buyers were mocked as **"kaneage zamurai" (money-raised
  samurai)** — you get the rank but **not the honour.** *Time caveat:* mainly a
  LATE-Edo (post-1780) phenomenon — at 1780 it's *emergent, not nationwide.* →
  **[DESIGN] a superb tradeoff: money CAN buy rank, but at a lasting reputation
  cost** — the anti-pay-to-win tension baked into history itself.
- **[FACT] Adoption (yōshi) was NOT a rags-to-samurai shortcut** — it preserved
  lineages and secured heirs under primogeniture, between roughly equal families
  (Moore 1970). → **[DESIGN] adoption is a lineage-CONTINUITY / heir-security
  mechanic, not a mobility cheat** (distinct from the kabu-purchase channel).

## 3 · Village = collective liability (a pressure system)

- **[FACT] Villages were self-administering and taxed COLLECTIVELY (murauke)** via a
  headman (**shōya** west / **nanushi** east / **kimoiri** Tōhoku), ~40–50% of
  harvest in rice, **not household-by-household.** The headman lineage is a concrete
  mid-game power rung.
- **[FACT] Collective liability ran into justice and control:** **gonin-gumi**
  (five-household groups) made members jointly responsible; punishment could fall on
  a whole village (enza); self-governing sanctions escalated **fines → severing ties
  → murahachibu (ostracism/expulsion).** → **[DESIGN] a rich cluster:** mutual
  surveillance, shared punishment, and a **graded ostracism ladder** as the
  village's enforcement of *reputation* — shared-fate pressure the player both
  suffers under and (later, as headman) wields.

## 4 · Domain & bakufu politics = factional risk

- **[FACT/INTERP] Domain politics turned on succession & faction (o-ie sōdō, "house
  strife")** — could escalate to violence, taken seriously by the shogunate.
  Retainers/kinsmen could **petition the shogunate directly**, dragging the bakufu
  into han politics (the Date Sōdō: local mediation → report to Edo → Tairō/Rōjū
  adjudication → a daimyō removed). *Caveat:* famous cases cluster in the 1660s–70s,
  ~a century pre-setting, but the *pattern* persists. → **[DESIGN] the escalation
  mechanic:** a household's fortunes are hostage to court intrigue; "call in the
  higher power" (appeal to Edo) both *resolves and endangers* a house — a
  high-stakes late-game move.
- **[DESIGN, under-verified] The fudai/tozama office gate is the T5 engine.** (See
  economy pass + open questions:) huge "outside" (tozama) lords were barred from
  bakufu office; small hereditary (fudai) lords held the top council seats. **Power
  at the top = court rank (kan'i) + office, not koku.** This is *why* the game's T5
  needs an office/favour track separate from koku. *Needs its own sourcing pass.*

## 5 · The Tenmei famine (from 1782) = a social-obligation shock, not just scarcity

- **[FACT] Relief flowed through status and obligation.** In Ono castle town,
  domain-funded rice-gruel kitchens (late 1783, run by town elders/guilds) were
  followed by town-wide pooled donations (early 1784) from wealthy merchants and
  purveyor guilds; the daimyō performed **"benevolent rule" by mobilising
  commoners' contributions**, charity hinging on donors' status ties to authority
  (Ehlers, *Monumenta Nipponica* 2014). → **[DESIGN] famine is a POLITICAL event:**
  benevolence is *performed* and *status-mediated* — relief buys legitimacy/favour;
  hoarding or failing to give costs reputation and risks riot (ikki/uchikowashi).

## 6 · Design synthesis — strongest mechanics for the rise

1. **Favour/patronage as the top-tier lever** (Tanuma) — the office track the human
   chose (full parallel track at T5). koku scales you; favour *places* you.
2. **Status tokens** (name, swords, gōshi rank) — make each rise *visible*, not
   just numeric; strong fit with the game's literary tone.
3. **Bought-rank-with-stigma** — money can buy status at a durable reputation cost;
   history's own anti-pay-to-win knob.
4. **Collective liability & the ostracism ladder** — the village tier's core
   tension (murauke, gonin-gumi, murahachibu); you suffer it low, wield it as
   headman.
5. **Factional escalation / appeal-to-Edo** — late-game risk: intrigue and the
   double-edged "call in the shogunate" move.
6. **Famine as a status-mediated obligation shock** — relief as performed
   benevolence that buys legitimacy; the setting's looming 1782 event.

**Preserve for authenticity:** the permeable-low / sealed-high asymmetry; status
tokens; the stigma on bought rank; collective village liability. **Abstract for
legibility:** the exact regional headman names (pick one); precise adoption law;
month-level famine sequencing; the o-ie-sōdō being century-displaced (use the
pattern, not the dates).

## 7 · Open questions (guide next design phase)

- **Ordinary early-game rungs below the samurai line** are thinly documented — the
  research has ceilings and the exceptional Tanuma case, but not the mundane
  labourer→servant→trusted-hand promotion timeline. (The game's own R0–R7 ladder
  already invents this — fine, but it's *design*, not history.)
- **giri / on / ninjō / gift-giving** as day-to-day mechanics produced *no direct
  claim* — central to the fantasy, still unsourced. A focused pass is warranted.
- **fudai/tozama × court-rank × sankin-kōtai** office-gating and fiscal drain is
  under-covered — the T5 office track's historical engine needs its own research.
- **Was gōshi purchase rare/stigmatised or available-but-costly at 1780
  specifically?** (It's emergent pre-1780, nationwide after.) Affects whether
  "buy rank" is an edge case or a real lane.
