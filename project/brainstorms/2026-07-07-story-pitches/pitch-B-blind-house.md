# Pitch: The Paper House

*(kami-kakushi — a story reboot pitch; anchor: the house drama is the spine)*

## 1. The premise, one page

In the winter of 1780 a man with no name walks out of the snow into the gateyard of the **Kamiya** house, in the village of **Kayanuma**, at the cold end of the **Kuzuhara valley**. He is fed once, out of form. He stays because there is wood to split.

The Kamiya — *paper house* — once held the valley's paper-making rights: mulberry fields, the winter vats, the license under the house's seal. Ten years ago the heir, in a famine winter, walked up the mountain path and did not come back. The village said *kami-kakushi* — the mountain took him — and the house let the village say it, because the truth it half-knew was worse to speak. Since that winter the house has been quietly dying: the paper-shed mortgaged to a soft-spoken town broker, the roof going, the old man writing smaller and smaller harvests into the day-book, a boy growing up on a story about his father that nobody in the house believes and nobody will correct.

This is a game about **raising a house back from the dead — and finding out that the corpse in its foundations is a lie it told on purpose.** The player is the nameless man. For sixty hours he works: hauls, splits, harvests, mends, carries, trades, fights off the boar and the road, learns the vats, carries the house's payments, and — deed by deed, in a world where worth only exists once it is *written* — climbs from the gateyard into the day-book, from the day-book into the house register, and from the servants' page, across six circles, toward the family's page. Every rung of his rise is a rung of the house's rise. And every layer of trust the house extends to him puts one more of its papers in his hands — because in this house, the paper *is* where the vanished man went.

The title is earned twice. *Kami-kakushi*, spirited away, is the lie the whole valley uses to bear its losses — and every vanishing the player untangles has a human floor: debt, flight, shame, arithmetic. And *kami* is a homophone the fiction leans on without ever saying aloud: **spirit and paper share a sound.** The heir was not hidden by gods. He was hidden by documents — an unstruck line in a register, a false receipt, a mortgage that bought a broker's silence. The player recovers him the only way this world recovers anyone: by name, in writing.

The stakes are the household, not the man. If the protagonist merely wanted a living, he could walk to town. What matters is that a house — its old man, its widow, its boy, its one ancient servant, its grandmother, its dignity — is being eaten from inside by the cost of a kindness it did once, silently, and can no longer afford. The player's labor is the only thing standing between the Kamiya name and the strike-through.

**Proper nouns are recastable.** *Kamiya* I would fight for — the paper/spirit pun is load-bearing and never spoken. Kayanuma, Kuzuhara, and all personal names below are placeholders chosen for plainness; rename freely.

## 2. The mystery spine

**What actually happened (the full truth, held by the design from day one).** In the famine winter of An'ei 2, the Kamiya could not feed six mouths through to thaw. The heir, **Naotarō**, did the arithmetic no one would speak, left his sandals pointing up the mountain, and walked into the snow — meaning to die so the others wouldn't. Charcoal burners in the next valley found him half-frozen and he lived — but by the time he could walk, Kayanuma had held his vanishing-rites and the house's neighbors had carried it rice out of pity for the *kami-kakushi*. Coming back would have unmasked the house as starving, its heir as a would-be suicide, its grief-alms as taken under false pretenses. He stayed. He became **Sasuke**, a burner. The broker **Rokubei**, who moves paper between the valleys, learned of it in the second year — and has been paid ever since: small sums, booked as "loans" and "prayer-fees," the gentlest blackmail in the province, the true engine of the house's debt. Inside the house, only the grandmother **O-Tose** knows he lives. The old man **Den'emon** suspects and refuses to know — which is why, in ten years, he has never struck his son's line from the register.

No spirit did anything. The mountain is just a mountain. The game keeps its one permitted ambiguity in its purse until the final scene (below).

**How the truth surfaces — and what the player does to earn it.** Every reveal is bought with the game's own currencies: deeds, standing, coin, and repetition.

- **T0 — the crack.** The player's grind up the household ladder is literally the earning of access: rung by rung the house lets him deeper — woodshed, doorstep, storehouse, day-book, register. Repeated storehouse labor (the discovery latch) surfaces the memorial alcove kept *wrong*: rites for the dead, but sandals set out facing away from the house, renewed each Bon. Capstone: at R7 the player is written into the house register — and on that same page sees Naotarō's line **unstruck**, no death date, ten years on. The house does not believe its own story. That is T0's whole payout, and the player buys it with roughly every deed the tier asks.
- **T1 — the contract taught.** The player solves a *different* vanishing first: O-Fuku's brother, gone thirty years. Earned by field-labor for her (deeds), a mountain expedition (combat/foraging on the high path), and market-work tracing an old pack-route. Truth in full: he fled a corvée levy and died in a landslide; his bones come home; he is mourned *by name*. Justice: the levy-officer is thirty years dead — never reached. The player learns what this game pays and what it doesn't. Her testimony, given in gratitude, adds one fact: charcoal smoke on the mountain the night Naotarō vanished, where no burner worked.
- **T2 — the paper.** Carrying the house's payments to town (a rung requirement — coin plus a trust the player has earned) puts Rokubei's receipts in his hands. Repeated dealings (the latch again — trades, errands, small favors banked) surface the wrong shape: the "loans" have no principal. Money flows *out* through Rokubei to somewhere north, twice a year, since An'ei 4. A parallel town case — an apprentice "spirited away," actually sold north by his master — is solved in full; the master walks, guild-protected. Partial justice, hard beat.
- **T3 — the man.** The trade route the player builds into the next valley (an estate/market project: reopening the charcoal contract the house let lapse) is the road to Sasuke. Recognition is earned, not granted: the field-guide's charcoal-lore, a burn-scar O-Sato once described, a way of stacking cords Kihei taught the heir. The player *finds him and does not confront him* — a DECIDE scene where every option is some grade of silence — and learns the vanishing's mechanics from the burners who saved him, by name, over a winter of bought trust.
- **T4 — the house itself.** The last holder of the truth is inside the walls. Standing high enough to be *owed* answers (the seasonal standing gate), the player brings the paper trail home. O-Tose's knowledge surfaces; Rokubei's hold breaks — not by ruin but by redemption of the mortgage the player's decade of surplus has made possible, and one private hour with the headman. Rokubei keeps his shop, his manners, his standing. He loses only the house. Some who deserve ruin walk; this one merely loses his grip.
- **T5 — the payout of the contract.** Full truth, formally: the house's *own* day-book — the private one, which the player now keeps — records what happened, all of it, in plain ink, so the house's memory can never again be blackmailed. The public register records the lawful minimum. Naotarō, offered his name back, declines to return: his return would spend the house's remaining dignity and his son's clean inheritance, and the House outlasts everyone in it. **The one ambiguity, spent here:** at the final Bon, a figure stands a while at the gate lantern and is gone before anyone reaches the yard. The game never says who. No spirit is implied. It is the only question the fiction ever leaves open.

## 3. The core cast

- **The protagonist** — no name; the game is the earning of one (gate → "the hand" → the house's use-name *Sakichi* → the register → the family's page). Wants, at first, only winter. Talks plainest of all: subject, verb, done. *Misread as:* everything, by everyone, in turn — that is the design.
- **Den'emon** (60, house head). Wants the name Kamiya to outlast him; suspects his son lives and has chosen not to know for ten years. *Misreads the protagonist* as a drifter angling at the will — trust comes only as written entries, never as words. *Shape:* short declaratives that end on nouns; he never asks a question.
- **O-Sato** (35, Naotarō's widow, runs the daily house). Wants her son to inherit clean of the debt. *Misreads the protagonist* as a rival for the boy's place, and is coldest exactly when he is most useful. *Shape:* everything conditional — "If the rice holds." "If the road is dry." Nothing in her mouth is ever certain.
- **Kihei** (55, the last servant). Wants to die in the house's service; carried Naotarō's last message to the mountain and has never said so. *Misreads the protagonist* as temporary — for two rungs he addresses him only by task: "Firewood." "Gate." *Shape:* imperatives built on tool-names; instructions first, reasons never.
- **Shinnosuke** (13, the heir's son). Wants his father to have been taken — the myth is kinder than being left. *Misreads the protagonist* as a man who may have crossed his father on some road, and interrogates him for it. *Shape:* questions stacked two at a time, never waiting for the first answer.
- **O-Tose** (78, grandmother). Knows. Wants the lie to hold until she is under the moss. *Misreads the protagonist* aloud and on purpose — as men from her girlhood, as the pedlar of forty years back — a confusion she wears as armor. *Shape:* speaks of present things in the past tense, as if all of it already happened long ago. **Sole maxim license in T0.**
- **Rokubei** (50s, town broker, from T1). Wants what he has: a gentle hand on every throat in two valleys. *Misreads the protagonist* as purchasable, and is patient about it. *Shape:* everything a favor — "Allow me." "It would ease me if."
- **Gonzaemon** (village headman). Wants no trouble that outlives him. *Misreads the protagonist* as Rokubei's plant inside the Kamiya. *Shape:* precedent — "In my father's time…" — every judgment an appeal backward.
- **O-Fuku** (neighbor farmwife). A sincere believer; her brother went thirty years ago and belief is how she has carried it — the game treats her gently. *Misreads the protagonist* as ill luck, a man the mountain sent back. *Shape:* agency always given to weather and mountain, never to people — "the path took him," "the snow decided."
- **Naotarō / "Sasuke"** (38, from T3). Wants his death to stay useful. Enters nameless — "the one who was taken" — and is recovered, by name, in ink.

## 4. The arc in circles

**T0 — The Gateyard (the household).** The house's standing: a name the village still bows to out of habit, a roof that leaks, a paper-shed under another man's seal. The line crossed at ascension: a servant is sent to speak *with the house's name in his mouth* — the moment a hired hand becomes the house's face. Mystery beat owned: the unstruck register line.

**T1 — The Village.** Standing: the Kamiya re-enter village reckoning — festival dues paid, a seat restored at the shrine council, the first season the standing assessment is said aloud without pity. Line crossed: from working *in* Kayanuma to being *answerable to it* — the house takes village obligations again, and the village's suspicion of its strange new man (Gonzaemon's misreading) is the toll. Beat owned: O-Fuku's brother recovered by name; the charcoal smoke.

**T2 — The Town.** Standing: the house deals on paper again — its seal re-registered, its debt confronted where debt lives. Line crossed: a peasant house negotiating with merchants as a principal, not a supplicant; the protagonist crosses from carrying coin to signing for it. Beat owned: Rokubei's shapeless loans; the apprentice case and its walking villain.

**T3 — The Next Valley.** Standing: the Kamiya hold paper on *others* now — the reopened charcoal contract makes them a creditor, and the standing score starts measuring what the house is owed. Line crossed: the valley line itself — roads, passes, strangers; the protagonist becomes the stranger arriving nameless at someone else's fire, and feels T0 from the other side. Beat owned: Sasuke found, known, and left unconfronted.

**T4 — The District.** Standing: the house heard at the intendant's office — by a clerk, never the lord; the ceiling holds. The paper-shed redeemed; the license home under the Kamiya seal. Line crossed: from petitioning power to being *entered in its books* — and inside the walls, the harder line: the truth spoken in the house for the first time, to O-Tose, who answers in past tense. Beat owned: the blackmail broken; partial justice served on Rokubei in a single quiet hour.

**T5 — The Succession.** Standing: the house whole, and therefore facing the only enemy left — time. Shinnosuke nears majority; the house must decide what the protagonist *is*. The late question is asked honestly — the man is offered a town post that would outgrow the house — and the answer honors the House: his line moves from the servants' page to the family's page as guardian, bridge, not owner. Beat owned: the full truth in the house's own book; Naotarō's choice; the lantern at the last Bon.

**T0 at rung level (R0→R7):**

- **R0 — The Gate.** Fed once, out of form. Actions: haul water, split wood; the log calls him "the man at the gate." Kihei says one word to him: "Firewood." Nothing is promised. The tier's tone is set: warmth costs, and no one here can afford it.
- **R1 — The Woodshed.** Lodging against labor. Kihei's task-lists open (hauling, mending fence, the boar-watch — first combat). He overhears the house's shape at night through the wall: O-Sato's conditionals, the boy's stacked questions. Gate: raw deeds, a survived boar-watch.
- **R2 — The House's Name in His Mouth.** First errand carrying the Kamiya name to a neighbor — and the village's first misreading of him, dramatized: doors answered halfway, O-Fuku's weather-talk. Gate: deeds plus a small coin float returned to the *mon*. Scene: Den'emon watches him count it back and says nothing that isn't a noun.
- **R3 — The Day-book.** A wage, entered in writing — the first time he exists on paper. The house gives him its use-name: *Sakichi*, the name every Kamiya hired man has carried, handed down like a tool. A name that is not his — and the first he's had. Gate: a season's recorded work. The day-book surface opens to the player here, dramatized as the thing it is.
- **R4 — The Storehouse Key.** Trusted with stores through the cold months. The discovery latch does its slow work: repeated storehouse labor surfaces the memorial alcove — rites for a dead man, sandals set out facing *away*, straw renewed this year. He says nothing. The log says less.
- **R5 — A Seat Inside.** Meals at the board, not the doorstep. Shinnosuke's interrogations begin; O-Sato's conditionals bend one degree ("If you're staying through planting —"). Gate: deeds plus the boy's errands done without complaint. The house is warming exactly as much as it can afford, which is barely.
- **R6 — The Payment Road.** Sent to town with the half-year's payment to Rokubei — coin the player ground for, handed over for *nothing the ledger can name*. He sees the receipt booked as "prayer-fees." First sight of the broker's shape: "Allow me." Gate: the coin itself, plus standing enough that Den'emon can risk being represented.
- **R7 — The Register.** Harvest accounted, roof-stage of the estate build done, the year survived. Den'emon opens the house register to enter a retained man, and asks his name; he has none he will claim; Den'emon writes *Sakichi* — "the house's name for you, then. Earn a better." And on the open page, three lines up: *Naotarō*. No strike. No death date. Ten years. Promotion scene, tier capstone, and the mystery's first crack — the same page, the same ink. Ascension is offered, opt-in, on the far side of it.

## 5. Fiction↔mechanics causation

The reboot's thesis: **the incremental grind and the house drama are the same object.** Deeds, the ladder, the estate, the standing score — all of it is the house's paper, and the mystery is *in* the paper.

**Required by this pitch:**

- **Deeds are the day-book.** Work-credit is not abstract: it is entries Kihei (later the player) keeps. The deed counter's fiction-name and surface follow FROM R3's scene, where the day-book opens because the story just put a wage in it. (Form is the fiction — the surface appears when the beat produces the need.)
- **Rungs are household standing.** R0→R7 must mean gateyard→register as above; promotion scenes are *acts of writing* (a task-list, a wage line, a key handed over, a register entry). The R7 scene must show the unstruck line — the rung gate and the mystery reveal are one screen.
- **The discovery latch is trust.** Hidden actions reveal through repetition because the house lets a man deeper *the longer he stays* — the mechanic already in the build is recast as the story's central social physics. The storehouse alcove (R4) and Rokubei's receipt-shapes (T2) ride it.
- **The estate build is the house's body.** Stages = roof, gate, storehouse, paper-shed redemption; each stage is a dignity beat, and stages can uncover things (what's under ten years of thatch). The standing score is the Kamiya name as the valley reckons it, re-assessed at harvest and festival — seasonal by fiction, not just by system.
- **No abstract game-word survives.** Coin in koku and mon; the bestiary is Kihei's vermin-and-mountain book, unlocked when he hands it over (mid-tier, as built); perks are *things and habits* — "a whetstone of your own," "the second-best sickle," "a seat nearer the fire."
- **The calendar carries the mystery.** Bon is an annual authored beat: the house's observance is wrong in a tellable, escalating way (the sandals; who renews them; who is seen doing it). The motif escalates per recurrence, never repeats verbatim.

**Suggested, not required:**

- Injury/recovery at home as *being tended* — trust dramatized through the comfort system (who sits with him scales with rung).
- The T3 route to the next valley as a market/estate project (the charcoal contract), so travel-to-the-truth is bought with the economy rather than merely walked.
- The register itself as a late DEV-visible surface — the family/servant page distinction doing endgame work at T5.
- One seeded RNG, pure core, no resets: all untouched; the NO-PRESTIGE law is this story's spine restated — *nothing rewinds, nobody forgets, the ink stays*.

## 6. One written sample scene + 5 log lines

**R7 — The Register.** *(Evening. The harvest counted. The lamp is the good one, brought out twice a year.)*

Den'emon set the register on the board and untied it the way another man handles a blade — by habit, and slightly away from himself. He found the servants' page. He inked the brush and held it, not writing.

"Your father's name for you."

Outside, Kihei was stacking the last cords; the sound came through the wall, four beats and a pause.

"I don't claim one."

Den'emon looked at him for the length of the pause. Then he wrote — three characters, unhurried — and turned the book so the wet ink faced its owner.

*Sakichi.*

"The house's name for you, then. Earn a better."

He reached for the blotting-cloth. The page lay open a moment longer than it needed to, and three lines up, in an older hand, a name stood with a birth-year beside it and nothing after. No strike. No date. The ink of it was ten years browner than the rest, and no fresher entry anywhere on the page had been allowed to touch its column.

Den'emon laid the cloth flat over the upper lines and pressed once.

"The lamp," he said, and it was to Sakichi, and it meant the scene was over.

He carried the lamp out. In the yard the stacking had stopped. Kihei stood with the last cord in his arms, looking up at the mountain the way a man looks at a door.

**Five log lines** *(the house journal; channels: work, house, market, mountain, rumor):*

- *Work* — Split wood till the light went. Kihei counted the cords twice, the second time slower. **+14 deeds.**
- *House* — A wage entered against the name Sakichi. First line in the day-book. The ink was let dry before the book was closed.
- *Market* — Sold hemp cord in Kayanuma, 82 mon. O-Fuku took her length last and paid without touching his hand.
- *Mountain* — Boar sign on the terrace edge, two nights old. The barley holds. It will not hold alone.
- *Rumor* — Talk at the well: the Kamiya keep a stranger past the thaw. Nobody said his name. Nobody has one to say.

## 7. Kernel self-score

1. **The lie of the vanishing** — every case floors on debt, flight, shame, or arithmetic; believers (O-Fuku, the boy) are held gently; the single ambiguity is budgeted, human, and spent at the last Bon — a strength, though it must be staged carefully to never read supernatural.
2. **Nobody twice unnamed** — the name-ladder (gate → the hand → Sakichi → register → family page) *is* the game; every vanished person is recovered in ink, by name; the lantern figure is the one deliberate edge-walk, and it is a question about *whether he came*, not who he is.
3. **Worth written by work** — literalized: deeds are a day-book, promotion is an act of writing, the protagonist is mediocre and rises only by recorded labor; warmth is priced (O-Sato's one degree) and never handed out unpaid.
4. **House before the man** *(the anchor)* — the mystery is the house's own wound, the debt is the price of the house's silence, the enemy attacks the ledger not the man, and the late question (the town post) is asked and answered in the house's favor: the family's page, as guardian, not owner.
5. **Partial justice** — a graded spread by design: Rokubei half-answers, the apprentice-seller walks, the levy-officer is unreachable; no throne room anywhere; the truth alone is paid in full, into the house's own book.
6. **Form is the fiction** — every surface enters through a scene that produces its need (day-book at R3, key at R4, vermin-book from Kihei's hands, register at R7); the strain is the attribute/perk layer, which must be dressed thing-by-thing and is the likeliest place a game-word could leak.
7. **The world is nobody's mirror** — every named character carries a specified misreading, several never fully warm (Gonzaemon, O-Sato's ceiling is one degree per tier), and the misreadings are dramatized as tolls: half-opened doors, coin counted back under watching eyes, a hand not touched at market.
