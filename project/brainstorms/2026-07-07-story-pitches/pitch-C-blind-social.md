# Pitch: THE ENTERED NAME

*A kami-kakushi story where the village is the protagonist's whole weather — and the player's real work is becoming someone the truth is safe to say to.*

## 1. The premise

In 1780, a man washes up under the bridge at Sugime, a village of forty houses in the Kagase valley. Iso the ferryman hauls him out of the Shirasu river like cargo. He has no papers, no name he will say, and one working idea: keep his head down and work.

The village has an older idea about people the river brings and people the mountain takes. Seven years ago, on the night House Kurehashi's granary burned, a bond-servant girl named Osae vanished from that house. Sugime says the kami took her. Her mother believes it because believing is how she gets up in the morning. The headman encourages it because the alternative is an audit. And House Kurehashi — once the valley's second name, now pitied, half-empty, in arrears — has been quietly bleeding standing ever since, because a house the gods rob is a house the gods have judged.

The protagonist is taken in by Kurehashi because the House needs hands and cannot afford to be choosy. He is not talented. He is not chosen. He is a stranger in a world where *stranger* is a durable, load-bearing category — and the game's sixty hours are the slow, witnessed, written-down labor of ceasing to be one. Not because the village is won over by his goodness. Because he becomes *routine*. Because his work enters ledgers, his habits get vouched for, his name — a name the village gives him, not the one he carries — gets entered in the register. And because a stranger cannot ask questions in Sugime, but a neighbor can, every rung of that ladder is also an inch closer to the granary fire, the shaved tally stick, and the girl the mountain never took.

The title is earned twice. *Kami-kakushi* is the lie Sugime tells itself about Osae — the mercy-story the whole social world is built to protect. And it is what the protagonist *is*: somewhere up-valley there is a village that tells his disappearance the same way, a mother who bears it the same way. He is a spiriting-away seen from the far side. The game is what happens when a walking kami-kakushi spends six tiers unpicking someone else's.

Why it matters for sixty hours: the incremental grind and the drama are the same act. In Sugime you cannot buy the truth and you cannot demand it. You can only become someone it is safe to say it to.

All proper nouns here — Sugime, Kagase, Shirasu, Kurehashi, and every villager — are invented and freely recastable; I chose plain, unglamorous names on purpose. The one I'd defend is the House's: **Kurehashi**, "dusk bridge" — a house at the crossing, in its evening.

## 2. The mystery spine

**What actually happened.** In a lean year, headman Denroku's office under-reported House Kurehashi's granary stores to the district intendant and sold the skim. Osae, a Kurehashi bond-servant, saw the tally sticks being shaved. When an audit threatened, the granary burned — fire set at the tally shelf, arithmetic made ash. Osae was not killed. Two men, one of them the woodcutter Kihachi, walked her over the pass by night with a little money and a warning: stay, and the fire is yours. She fled and lived. Denroku let the kami-kakushi story grow because it soothed the mother, closed the questions, and cost only Kurehashi — who absorbed the loss, the debt, and the quiet stain of being god-punished. Cold arithmetic, debt, shame, flight: every part of it human. The district clerk, Mizoguchi, who pocketed the largest share, was promoted away within the year.

**How truth surfaces — and what the player does to earn each piece:**

- **T0.** The village investigates *him* — the player spends the first tier as Sugime's open case, feeling the machinery of suspicion from inside. Meanwhile, clearing the burned granary (estate labor, banked deeds) latches the first physical discovery: char deepest at the tally shelf, and one scorched tally stick, shaved. Earned by work, found by working.
- **T1.** Rebuilding the granary stage by stage exposes the burn pattern: set from inside. Trusted at last with Jinbei's old house journals (a rung-gated unlock), the player finds the week of the fire cut out — pages missing from a man who never skips a day. Earned by trust the House extends because it needs him, not because it likes him.
- **T2.** Kihachi. No interrogation verb exists in this game. The player cuts wood beside him, season after season — proximity as mechanic, the discovery latch worn down by shared labor — until the sentence Kihachi never finishes finally loses its last wall: Osae left *alive*. A DECIDE governs where he says it — pressed at the fire in front of the village, or on the mountain alone — same truth, very different cost to Kihachi and to the player's standing.
- **T3.** Paper. Trading under the House's recovered seal, the player reaches the market-town brokers, buys and barters access to old grain receipts, and assembles the audit that never happened. At the district office he glimpses Mizoguchi once — sleek, busy, forgettable — through a doorway, and is not admitted. That is the whole scene. That is the ceiling.
- **T4.** Osae. The pass opens with the tier. Trail-shrines, an inn register two valleys over, a woman called Omitsu with a husband and a child and a flinch she has almost retired. She confirms everything, signs nothing, and will not come back. The player carries her mother's message up the mountain and carries the answer down, and the answer is no. She is recovered *by name* — the game's contract — and the name is given back to the record, to her mother, to the House. The person keeps her new one.
- **T5.** The reckoning. The assembled truth is read into the district record: the fraud pinned to the headman's office, the House's ledger cleared, its standing rewritten. Denroku does not stand trial; he is dying, and confesses only to Hōgetsu, who tells no one. Kihachi answers in full — the smallest man pays the most. Mizoguchi is never reached. Full truth, partial justice, exactly as promised. The last thread is the protagonist's own: the player learns (in quiet T4–T5 scenes) what he fled — a bonded second son of a sold-out house, who let his village believe the mountain took him to spare his mother the shame of a runaway. The final DECIDE: send word home that he lives, or let the mercy-lie stand. He now knows precisely what each choice does to a mother. The game does not grade it.

**The one permitted ambiguity** is spent small and early: Otoku says that on the night Osae vanished she heard the mountain breathe her daughter's name. Nothing in the game ever confirms or dissolves that sound. Everything else is paper.

## 3. The core cast

- **Jinbei** — head of House Kurehashi, near-blind, keeper of the house journal (the game's log is literally his book, and later the protagonist's hand takes it over). *Wants:* the House to outlast him. *Misreads the protagonist* as a debt-runner who'll vanish with the spring — extends trust anyway, because the House needs hands. *Shape:* ledger entries — flat declaratives, subject dropped, cut before the sentiment. "Roof held. That's the year's luck spent."
- **Sana** — Jinbei's granddaughter; keeps the real accounts behind his failing eyes. *Wants:* solvency without marrying out to get it. *Misreads him* as another mouth, then as her grandfather's soft spot; she is the one who never fully warms — respect, yes; welcome, never quite. *Shape:* a number in every sentence — she counts and prices everything, including feelings. "Two winters you've eaten here. Sixty mon of you is still salt we lent."
- **Otoku** — Osae's mother, fieldworker for the House. *Wants:* her daughter's vanishing to mean something. *Misreads him* as the river's trade — something sent back in exchange for what the mountain took — and her warmth arrives early and for the wrong reason, which is its own knife. *Shape:* everything paired — two clauses, the second one darker. *Licensed epigram holder.*
- **Denroku** — village headman; brokered the cover-up; publicly pious. *Wants:* to die respected. *Misreads him* first as a tool (an unregistered man is leverage), then as weather turning. *Shape:* never says "I" — the village is his grammatical subject. "We take in what we can carry. We are not a large village."
- **Gonsuke** — neighboring farmer, the gossip node; friendly to the protagonist's face, reporting on him by dark. Not malicious — talk is his currency. *Wants:* to matter at the fire. *Misreads him* constantly and retails every version. *Shape:* half his sentences are quotations — "Iso says," "they're saying" — he never owns a claim.
- **Hōgetsu** — the shrine keeper; curator of the valley's kami-kakushi stories; knows the lie is a lie and tends it as mercy. *Wants:* the village's grief to stay bearable. *Misreads him* as an omen to be read rather than a man to be known. *Shape:* short questions, and the game's other *licensed epigram holder* — at most one maxim, rarely spent.
- **Iso** — ferryman and bridge keeper; pulled the protagonist from the Shirasu. *Wants:* the bridge kept and to be left alone. *Misreads him least and remembers him least* — treats him permanently as cargo once hauled. *Shape:* past tense for everything, even the present. "You were standing on my bridge."
- **Kihachi** — woodcutter, drinker; one of the two who walked Osae over the pass. *Wants:* to be already punished. *Misreads him* as judgment finally arriving. *Shape:* never finishes the sentence that matters — stops one word short, and no one else in the game is permitted that gesture.
- **Mizoguchi** — the district clerk. Glimpsed once, through a doorway, mid-errand. No wants on screen. That is the point of him.

The protagonist talks plainest of all: short, concrete, no figures of speech. The village supplies his descriptions; he supplies work.

## 4. The arc in circles

**T0 — Hands.** The House is pitied: half-empty rooms, arrears, a burned granary nobody clears because clearing it means looking at it. The social line ascension crosses: *unperson → person in law* — from a body the river left to a name entered in the village register. The mystery beat T0 owns: the village's investigation of *him* (suspicion as playable weather), and the granary's first secret — the shaved tally stick. *(Rung detail below.)*

**T1 — Roof.** The House is solvent for one winter and dares to say so quietly. The line crossed: *servant → household* — he eats inside, and the house journal passes into his hand, which is the game's log changing narrators mid-story. Mystery beat: the fire was set from inside, and the week of the fire is razored out of Jinbei's old journals — the House has been protecting itself from its own record.

**T2 — Gate.** The House lends instead of borrows; neighbors come to *its* door. The line crossed: *resident → voice* — the tier ends the first time Gonsuke quotes the protagonist to someone else, which in Sugime is citizenship. Mystery beat: Kihachi's unfinished sentence finishes. Osae left alive. The lie stops being a mystery and becomes a decision everyone alive that night once made.

**T3 — Road.** The House trades under its own seal again; its name travels the valley faster than its people do. The line crossed: *village name → valley name* — and misreading returns at scale: in a market town, a stranger tells the protagonist the folktale of the drowned man of Sugime, wrong in every detail, and he pays for his soup and lets it stand. Mystery beat: the paper trail assembled; Mizoguchi glimpsed once; the ceiling touched.

**T4 — Pass.** The House is consulted — Sana speaks at the district grain-meet, and is heard. The line crossed: *the House's name goes where its people cannot* — and the protagonist crosses the pass in person, out of everyone's watching, for the first time since the river. Mystery beat: Osae found, alive, renamed, unwilling. Full truth; the first installment of partial justice is learning that recovery and return are different things.

**T5 — The Name.** The House stands re-assessed at the valley's reckoning: solvent, honored, continuing. The line crossed is the last one: *the man is asked whether he has outgrown the House* — there is an offer from beyond the valley, and the drama is real — and the answer honors the House: he is written in as its steward-heir, because the only name he has ever actually owned is one a house wrote down. Mystery beat: the truth entered in the district record; the House's stain lifted in ink; Otoku given her daughter's name back and her mercy-lie dismantled as gently as the game knows how; his own vanishing answered by his own hand, one way or the other.

### T0 at rung level (R0→R7)

Every rung title is *what Sugime calls him* — the ladder is the village's mouth. Every promotion scene is witnessed: someone says the new name aloud; nothing promotes in private.

- **R0 — The drowned man.** He wakes in Kurehashi's outbuilding, owing his life to a ferryman who's already half forgotten him. Actions: haul water, mend the byre wall, split kindling — labor that banks deeds *while Gonsuke happens to pass, twice, going nowhere*. The log opens as Jinbei's journal; its first entry about the player is not flattering. **Gate:** first deeds banked, first meal worked off. **Promotion:** Jinbei writes him in — "the man from the river" — and the journal becomes a surface the player checks, because it is being kept *about him*.
- **R1 — The river man.** Field access; the map's first edges (fields, riverbank, the bridge). Hidden action at the riverbank: searching the reeds, attempts accruing, hints tightening — until his own washed-up bundle surfaces: a cord, a bond-paper too water-ruined to read, which the player may burn or keep. The market opens and sells to him at stranger's prices — a visible, mechanical distrust surcharge. Nobody explains it. Nobody has to. **Gate:** field deeds + first coin + the riverbank latch sprung.
- **R2 — Kurehashi's stray.** Gonsuke's version of him reaches every fire in the village, and it is wrong: a runaway samurai's groom, a debtor, a fire-setter — the DECIDE lets him contradict it, let it stand, or feed a better lie, each with distinct standing and market consequences that play out over weeks. First crafting: he repairs the House's second scythe rather than buying one, and Sana prices the repair aloud, to the mon. **Gate:** a repaired thing + coin + the rumor DECIDE resolved.
- **R3 — The hands.** The House's first real trust, given as work: Sana assigns him the burned granary, seven years untouched. Clearing it is the estate build's first stage — rubble hauled, beams sorted, estate deeds banked — and it springs T0's central latch: char deepest at the tally shelf, and in the ash, one tally stick, shaved. He shows Jinbei. Jinbei looks at it a long time and says, "Granary's clear. Good." The journal that night records the clearing and not the stick. The player has just watched the log *withhold* — the House's book protecting the House. **Gate:** estate stage one complete.
- **R4 — The borrowed man.** Other houses start asking Kurehashi for the loan of him — the map opens village-wide, labor diversifies (woodcutting up the slope beside a man named Kihachi, who works hard and finishes nothing he starts saying). First mountain trouble: a boar in the lower fields; the House lends him the mountain-book — the field-guide surface arriving as an heirloom, not a menu. **Gate:** deeds banked for three different houses + the boar answered.
- **R5 — The one Otoku feeds.** Otoku begins leaving food at the outbuilding door, never knocking. The home surface deepens — comfort, recovery, a mended quilt — and so does the misreading: she feeds him because the river sent him, a trade for her girl, and when she finally says so, plainly and kindly, the player has no dialogue option that fixes it. Being loved wrongly is not a problem the game solves. **Gate:** home comfort threshold + Otoku's scene witnessed.
- **R6 — The man at the fire.** He is allowed at the winter fire-talk — and the log gains its hearth channel: village news arriving firsthand instead of secondhand and late. The first thing he hears at the fire is the kami-kakushi story of Osae, told whole, by Hōgetsu, with Otoku present — told *as mercy*, and received as such by everyone but the man holding a shaved tally stick in his memory. **Gate:** standing + a winter survived.
- **R7 — The entered name.** To stay past a second year he must be entered in the population register, and the headman must sign. Denroku names his price in "we": the House answers for him, in writing, against any harm he ever does — Kurehashi mortgages standing it barely has, on a stranger, because the House needs hands and Jinbei has decided. The capstone DECIDE is the name itself: the village's convenience-name, or the name the House offers — **Yosuke, of Kurehashi** — a name that is the House's before it is his. The tier ends with a brush, a register, and the ladder's whole meaning made literal: he exists now, because it is written. Ascension to T1 is opt-in from here, as a story event: the first night he eats *inside*.

## 5. Fiction↔mechanics causation

The pitch's engine: **in Sugime, nothing is true until it is witnessed, and nothing is durable until it is written.** Every incremental system maps onto one of those two verbs.

**REQUIRED by this pitch:**

- **Rung titles are village-voiced names** ("the drowned man" → "the entered name"), and every promotion scene is a witnessed social scene — someone says the name aloud. The ladder is not the player's self-assessment; it is Sugime's mouth. (Reuses the existing rung/promotion structure; changes only what rungs *mean*.)
- **Distrust is a felt mechanical cost in T0:** a stranger's surcharge at the market and access-gating on nodes/actions by rung, decaying as rungs rise. Kernel #7's application clause, made of numbers. (Market modifier + existing rung-gating.)
- **The log is the house journal, with a narrator:** Jinbei's voice in T0, the protagonist's hand from T1 — and it is capable of *withholding* (R3), because it is a house's book, not a god's. Channels are the journal's sections: house matters, hearth-talk, the mountain, the market. The hearth channel *unlocks at R6 as a story beat* — village news firsthand is a privilege, not a default.
- **The register scene as T0 capstone DECIDE** — the name choice is the tier's payoff and the House-before-the-man thesis in one screen.
- **Discovery latches are social latches:** hidden actions reveal through *presence* — the village stops seeing him, so it stops hiding — and the tightening hint text is written as gossip-drips and half-sentences (Kihachi's mechanic and his sentence shape are the same fact).

**SUGGESTED (works without, sings with):**

- **Deeds framed as witnessed work** — flavor, not new logic: banking deeds narrates who saw.
- **Auto-repeat as "habit," diegetically:** the toggle's fiction is routine, and routine is what villagers cite when they vouch — "he's at the ditch every morning" is both the mechanic and the testimony. The most incremental-native trust system I can offer: predictability as currency.
- **The seasonal standing re-assessment staged as the valley reckoning** — a scene, twice a year, where Sugime re-prices every house aloud and the House's number moves in public.
- **Attributes wear period names** (Arm, Breath, Eye, Tongue, Patience) and **perks are things or habits per prose law #3:** *Iso's Knot. The Second Scythe. Otoku's Kitchen. The Whetstone Habit.*
- **Combat's fiction:** boar, dogs, weather-work, and men on roads — the field-guide a dead son's mountain-book, inherited mid-tier as trust made paper.
- **Timed actions as propriety:** cooldowns are the rules of calling on people — you do not knock twice in a day.

Nothing here resets, and the pitch leans into that hard: NPCs' misreadings persist across tiers *because* nothing rewinds — Gonsuke's wrong story from R2 is still circulating, mutated, when a market-town stranger retails it back to the player in T3.

## 6. Sample scene + five log lines

### The Entered Name *(T0, R7 promotion — the headman's house, second month, snow going gray)*

The register lay open on the low table. Denroku had warmed the ink himself, which Jinbei later said was the most work the man had done all winter.

"We take in what we can carry," Denroku said. "We are not a large village. We have carried this man two years without a name to carry him by."

"He's carried grain both years," Jinbei said. "Weighed same as anyone's."

"We don't doubt the grain. We ask who answers, if there is ever answering to do."

"Kurehashi answers."

It was said before the protagonist could open his mouth, and it was said the way Jinbei said everything, like a line already entered, dry before anyone could argue with the ink. Sana, by the door, looked at her grandfather the way she looked at a column that wouldn't sum. She said nothing. She was counting what it cost, and it did not sum, and she said nothing.

Denroku turned the register toward them. "Then a name. We write what he is to be called, not what he was."

The protagonist looked at the page. Forty houses, and every soul in them, in one book.

"Yosuke," Jinbei said. "There was a Yosuke of Kurehashi in my grandfather's time. Good with ditches. The name's been idle."

"It's not mine," the protagonist said.

"No. It's the House's. You'd be borrowing it."

Outside, someone's ox complained about the cold, and up the lane a door slid shut — Gonsuke's, by the sound, closing on everything he'd seen through the gap. By morning the village would know the stranger had a name, and by evening it would know three versions of how he got it.

The brush belonged to the headman. The hand that took it did not shake, or it did; the register only records that on the fourth day of the second month, Yosuke, of Kurehashi, was entered.

Jinbei was already at the door, working his feet into his sandals.

"Ditches, come thaw," he said. "Earn it idle no longer."

## 7. Kernel self-score

1. **The lie of the vanishing.** The lie is the pitch's load-bearing wall: kami-kakushi as the village's collective alibi for cold arithmetic; believers (Otoku, Hōgetsu) treated as grieving, never mocked; the one ambiguity spent in a single uncontradicted sound. No yokai, ever. *Honored.*
2. **Nobody twice unnamed.** The protagonist's arc *is* this law mechanized — the rung ladder is the village naming him by degrees; Osae is recovered by name even when the person refuses recovery. Strain: his birth name may go unsaid — I read the law as binding what the story touches twice, and the story deliberately touches his old name once, as his choice to make. *Honored, with one deliberate edge.*
3. **Worth written by work.** Deeds witnessed, standing re-priced in public, existence itself conferred by a register entry; he is mediocre by design and named after a man good with ditches. *Honored.*
4. **House before the man.** The House mortgages its standing to buy him a name; his name is literally the House's, borrowed; T5's outgrow-the-House question is asked and answered in the House's favor. *Honored.*
5. **Partial justice.** Kihachi (smallest) pays fullest; Denroku dies confessed but unjudged; Mizoguchi is one doorway-glimpse and a promotion; the only justice that fully lands is the ledger kind, done to the House's record. Truth arrives complete. *Honored.*
6. **Form is the fiction.** Log = house journal with a narrator who can withhold; bestiary = an inherited mountain-book; banzuke = the valley reckoning; every surface arrives as a dramatized need (hearth channel at R6, field-guide as heirloom). *Honored.*
7. **The world is nobody's mirror** *(anchor)*. The village investigates, surcharges, misquotes, and mis-loves him; Otoku's warmth is built on a mistake the game never lets him correct; Sana never fully warms; Iso forgets him; his own story returns to him as garbled folklore. Misreading is priced in mon and gates, not narrated. *This is the pitch's spine — honored hardest.*
