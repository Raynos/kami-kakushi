## Locked creative constraints (from the human, 2026-06-25)

These are **non-negotiable** and every story beat below honors them (see ADRs D-001…D-005):

1. **Grounded, not magical.** Amnesia has a mundane cause. No tengu, spirits, reincarnation, isekai, or magic.
2. **Pure Edo folk-mystery tone.** Folklore appears only as what people *believe & fear* — superstition,
   rumor, ritual, atmosphere. Every mystery resolves to a human/natural cause. Thin ambiguity allowed
   for unease; never confirmed magic; **never magic powers for the player**.
3. **Mediocre start.** A normal, average, even unremarkable 17-year-old farmhand. No hidden power, no
   secret training, no bloodline. Growth comes **only** through perseverance, repetition, and refusing
   to quit. Zero-to-competent-through-effort *is* the core fantasy.
4. **Setting.** A small rural lower-samurai (*goshi*) estate and its valley in Edo-period Japan; the
   village explains the protagonist's arrival with the *kamikakushi* ("spirited away") folk-belief, but
   the truth is mundane and human.
5. **One grounded diegetic reset** late-game carrying meta-progress forward (a season-cycle time-skip /
   founding a venture), **not** literal reincarnation or a magic time-loop.
6. It is an **incremental RPG**: peaceful Edo labor is the daily texture and numbers-go-up economy;
   idle auto-resolving combat is the exception; the story motivates the gradual unlocking of systems.

Working title: **Kamikakushi** (神隠し).

## Premise (one line)

A mediocre 17-year-old farmhand wakes injured and nameless at a remote lower-samurai estate where a
grieving valley is certain he is **Tama**, the child "spirited away by the kami" ten years ago — and
through nothing but daily toil and a refusal to quit he must rebuild himself from useless to
barely-capable while uncovering the wholly human truths the kamikakushi legend was invented to hide:
who he actually is, what really happened to the real Tama, and why the road and river that nearly
killed him still run, lethal and lied-about, right past his new home.

## How the title fits a grounded truth

"Kamikakushi" is the word **Asagiri valley** reaches for whenever someone vanishes — and the valley
already has its sacred case: a child, **Tama**, "taken by the mountain god" from the **Kurosawa** goshi
estate's lands a decade ago, mourned every Bon with rice and a child's straw sandal at the mountain
shrine. So when a half-drowned, memoryless 17-year-old washes up below the estate after a flood, the
village doesn't see a stranger — it sees the kami **giving Tama back**. The title is the valley's first
and fondest lie, and it lies on three peeled-back layers:

1. The protagonist wasn't spirited away — he was concussed and swept downriver by an ordinary flood.
2. The original Tama wasn't taken by any god — she was driven out by human ugliness (a debt, a violent
   house, adults who let a comforting spirit-story stand because the truth shamed them).
3. "Hidden by the kami" is, by the end, the exact phrase powerful men use to bury what they let happen:
   a drowned upstream hamlet "the gods punished" was in fact a neglected embankment quietly sacrificed
   to protect tax fields.

The same word — for grief, for shame, for negligence dressed as fate — names every mystery in the game,
and not one of them is the work of a spirit.

## The grounded amnesia cause

Blunt head trauma + near-drowning + exposure — all medically real. A neglected upstream embankment
failed on a rain-swollen autumn night; a flash flood tore through the mountain pass and the porters'
caravan he traveled with. He took a rock/beam to the skull, half-drowned, was swept downriver, and
snagged at a weir below the Kurosawa estate where a charcoal-burner found him at dawn. Ordinary
retrograde amnesia (concussion + hypoxia + fever): he keeps language and bodily habits (ties a porter's
load-knot without thinking) but has lost his autobiographical past. The estate herbalist dresses a
healing scalp gash that physically grounds the injury. Memory returns never in a magic flash but in slow
mundane sensory fragments (paddy-mud smell, a familiar road, a name surfacing in a recurring
half-drowning dream) — and only ever restores an ordinary, sad past, **never any hidden skill**.

## The central mystery (four nested human questions)

The valley's supernatural certainty is the red herring blocking every mundane answer:

1. **Am I Tama?** The village insists the kami returned their lost child.
2. **If not, who am I?** The calluses, the porter's knots, the name in the water demand an origin.
3. **What really happened to the real Tama?** If he isn't her, the original disappearance reopens as an
   unsolved human case.
4. **Why does the valley (and the power above it) need this buried?** The same flood, road, and officials
   that nearly killed him still move rice and silver, still calling their crimes "the will of the kami."

The pull: the answers aren't a distant quest but physically nearby and tightening — the more competent
and curious he becomes, the more he becomes a problem to people who liked him better as a ghost.

## The grounded resolution

**He is NOT Tama.** The resemblance is real but coincidental and suggestible (right age, similar build,
a birthmark near the same place); a decade of grief and guilt did the rest, and the legend has even
drifted on details (a fair-play clue).

His true identity, recovered piece by mundane piece: **Ren**, an orphaned tenant-farmhand-turned-porter
from **Kuzuhara**, an upstream hamlet, indentured to a transport guild to work off a dead parent's debt.

**Kuzuhara didn't "anger the gods."** During a famine year the district deputy magistrate's office,
pressed for its rice quota, quietly diverted the corvée labor and relief-rice that maintained the
upstream embankment to protect the well-kept downstream dykes guarding the tax fields and its own
interests; a frightened local headman doctored the ledgers to cover the shortfall. When the monsoon
came, the undermined *seki* failed in the night and the poorest, lowest hamlet drowned while the
protected fields held — negligence shading into a chosen sacrifice of the expendable, then blamed on the
kami and on an "unlucky, impious" village. Ren was the porter on a guild caravan caught in that same
flood; the guild wrote him off because the route was running short-weighted tax rice and smuggled
silver, and admitting the man meant admitting the contraband.

**The real Tama was never taken by anything.** Tama (a *girl*, not the boy the legend "remembers" — the
gender drift is a clue) ran from a violent stepfather and a household that had agreed to sell her into
service to clear a rice debt owed to the Kurosawa estate; a midwife and a traveling peddler helped her
flee down-valley, and she has been alive and grown the whole decade, working in a post-town, too afraid
and ashamed to return. The adults who knew let "the kami took her" stand because a tragic miracle is
easier to live with than "we were about to sell a child."

**The estate is bound into it too:** the rice debt that nearly sold Tama is the same debt-and-quota
machine that drowned Kuzuhara — the estate's old guilt and the magistrate's racket are one rotten root.

**Resolution is human and partial — never vengeance, never a clean hero's win.** Ren can undo none of
it. He survives, recovers his ordinary sad past, finds the few scattered survivors (chiefly Kiku),
reunites the valley with the living, grown Tama, and forces the buried truths open through evidence and
testimony (a doctored ledger, a surviving corvée tally-stick, a guilt-sick clerk, the rigged *masu*
measuring-box, a porter-witness). The reachable culprits answer (headman, steward, enforcer); the truly
untouchable magistrate largely escapes — *because that is how the world is* — but Kuzuhara's drowned are
named at last, Tama is freed to choose, and Ren claims a true name and a life he built by hand instead
of the borrowed miracle he was handed.

## Folklore as believed-atmosphere (the belief → cause table)

Folklore is the valley's emotional weather and conscience-cover — never its physics — and is treated
respectfully (how powerless, grieving people make the unbearable bearable). Each belief is true to the
heart that holds it and false to the world; the game lets the player *feel* the dread before showing the
cause, and reveals with restraint (no Scooby-Doo unmasking):

| Folk-belief | Grounded cause |
| --- | --- |
| **Kamikakushi** (the master lie) | A flood victim (Ren); a child who *ran* (Tama); negligence dressed as the kami's punishment (Kuzuhara) |
| Weir/ford **kappa** | A real undertow + undercut bank; a poacher's weighted nets; the smugglers' sinking-spot for evidence and drowned witnesses |
| **Tanuki** stealing rice | A skimming foreman feeding the racket |
| **Fox-fire** / ghost-lights on the ridge | A charcoal-burner's hidden kiln; later, the magistrate's men moving forbidden ground at night |
| **One-eyed mountain god** of the pass | Hanzaki, the scarred ronin enforcer's lookout |
| **Yamanba** of the high woods | A flood-burned widow surviving feral — "defeated" by being fed & resettled (a mercy quest, not a kill) |
| **Tengu** of the high cedars | Genza, a fugitive ex-foot-soldier who becomes a wary teacher & witness |
| "The night the road walks" / spirited-away | Landslide-prone, fog-blind terrain where travelers genuinely die (how Ren arrived) + people who fled debt/conscription/violence/shame |
| Yearly **soul-calling rite** | Grief-work that nonetheless yields hard evidence (the priest's register of vanished travelers ties disappearances to the road) |

**Retained ambiguity (atmospheric only, never mechanical):** a recurring half-drowning dream of a voice
calling his name (a real survivor, not a spirit); a twice-helpful fox; one shrine omen too neat; an
unidentified hand leaving an offering at the boundary-stone. A shiver, never a spell, and no yokai is
ever shown to be real or to grant anything. *(See "Refinements to apply" — harden these so they can't
read as confirmed magic.)*

## Mediocre start, honored mechanically

He is plainly, mechanically ordinary from minute one, and the game says so in text and numbers. The
opening "gameplay" is literally convalescence (resting, drinking water, being spoon-fed millet gruel)
because he is a concussed, feverish invalid. When he can work, his hands blister on the first hoe; he is
slower than the ten-year-old who shows him the paddy; the steward calls him "another mouth, soft and
clumsy, eats more than he earns." **No hidden edge to discover** — no bloodline, no secret training, no
sword that answers to him, no body that "remembers" combat. Every returning memory is ordinary hard
labor and ordinary fear. The load-knot habit is explicitly a *laborer's*, not a warrior's ("huh, you've
hauled before"). The one thing the village treats as special — that he's the returned Tama — is **false**.
His only genuine asset is temperament: stubbornness, a strong back, a patient temper, a refusal to stop
getting up — exactly what a good laborer needs and what the incremental grind rewards.

## Growth through effort (the training-montage economy)

Every number that goes up is sweat the player chose to spend, and the story names it relentlessly.
Stamina, strength, a calloused-hands stat, and skill ranks (Hoeing, Foraging, Woodcutting, Fishing,
Carrying, later Spear/Staff) rise only through thousands of repetitions with diminishing returns you
push past only by showing up daily. Combat capacity is **gated behind labor-built conditioning** — you
can't stand against a bandit until months of hauling rice bales have given you a back and wind; the
spear is taught as "just a long hoe held with intent." The mentor's creed (stated early, paid off in the
finale): **"Talent is a story the lucky tell. You are not lucky. So you will work."** Plateaus are real;
the answer to a wall is never a magic item but more reps, better technique, more rest, better tools you
crafted. Overtraining injuries force rest. Even the mystery is bought with grind — village trust (a
tracked reputation earned by completed labor and survived seasons) unlocks the confessions that crack
the case. By endgame he is genuinely capable, but the game shows it took a year of dawns — the player
feels they **built** this person rather than discovered a chosen one. The meta-progress that survives
the late-game reset is exactly this hard-won competence and standing.

## Combat framing & the humbling first fight

Early enemies are mundane countryside threats — a boar wrecking the millet, monkeys raiding stores, a fox
in the henhouse, a rabid stray-dog pack, leeches/hornets/a viper — escalating to human danger (a lone
desperate rice-thief, then famine-driven bandits) and the genuinely lethal mountains (cold, falls, fog,
hunger-maddened animals). Every "yokai" is one of these wearing folklore's costume.

**The humbling first fight:** weeks in, cornered on the wood-gathering trail or defending the grain
store, he swings a carrying-pole or hatchet, lands at most one lucky blow, then is thrashed — disarmed,
ribs cracked, robbed of the tool, left in the dirt — surviving only by luck or because Jūbei arrives,
**never by skill**. The game states the lesson outright ("you swing like you're chasing crows from the
rice; the blade is heavier than any hoe, and he is faster and stronger and trained, and you are not").
The shame of limping home to confess he lost the estate's tool is the engine that drives him to beg for
drills. Combat stays idle/auto-resolving afterward, but outcomes are explicitly a function of
conditioning and skill ground out through labor — for a long time the survivable play is to avoid, flee,
or fight in numbers; he wins later only because he trained.

## Act structure

- **Act 0 — The Weir (Prologue / onboarding).** Wakes feverish and nameless in a storehouse; pulled
  half-drowned from the river with a dressed head wound and hands that know labor he can't remember.
  Sayo names him "Tama" on sight; word races that the kami returned the spirited-away child. UI near-bare
  & diegetic (body/rest bar, rice counter, one or two survival tasks) because he is an invalid earning
  his keep. Seeds: the first half-drowning dream; the wrongness of a whole village treating a stranger as
  a miracle while he can barely lift a hoe. Ends when he survives his first task and earns a
  sleeping-place — he has **chosen** to stay and find out who he is.
- **Act 1 — Earning Your Rice (the labor economy unlocks).** Recovery through repetition. Season by
  season he becomes a trusted hand as farming → foraging → woodcutting → fishing → basic crafting unlock
  one panel at a time, each gated diegetically by rising stamina and NPCs literally granting access. The
  koku economy comes online. Folklore texture thickens (soul-calling rite, feared ford, the "tanuki,"
  offerings at the stone where he was found). He grows attached — to Jūbei, the herbalist, and especially
  **Sayo**, whose hope that he *is* her lost friend is the warm hook and the engine of the red herring.
  Threads: the recurring dream-name, flashes of paddies that aren't Asagiri's, the off-limits "cursed"
  upstream valley. Act ends with the humbling near-fatal fight.
- **Act 2 — Calluses (training & the first threads).** Shamed by the beating, he begs Jūbei for drills;
  the training/combat/conditioning systems unlock as a grind loop he keeps failing, and idle auto-combat
  against vermin and lesser mountain dangers opens — all gated behind labor-built stamina. Investigating
  the thieves and the "tanuki" leads to a skimming foreman and the first hard fact: the road moves more
  rice than the books admit. He meets the grounded "yokai" as rumor. A manifest scrap, a porter's-knot
  habit, a debt-token surface; the dream-name resolves to "Kuzuhara." The magistrate's man takes a
  too-keen interest; the legend stops adding up (different ages, even a different gender remembered). The
  question sharpens from "who am I" to "what happened up the valley."
- **Act 3 — The Things People Remember (investigation & the mountains).** Now capable enough to range, he
  pushes into the forbidden upstream ruins and the dangerous mountains, unlocking deep gathering,
  expeditions/logistics, and an investigation layer. The "haunting" resolves into a feral survivor,
  **Kiku**, who recognizes him (both Kuzuhara children). Together they uncover the disaster's shape (the
  broken seki, the bones, the burned relief-storehouse) and the darker turn (the embankment was neglected
  and the failure covered). The kappa-ford resolves (weighted evidence, a corpse); the one-eyed god
  resolves (the enforcer's lookout). The legend collapses fairly — proof Tama was a *girl* who *ran* —
  the gut-punch that detonates the village's miracle. The two buried lies (the estate's near-sale of
  Tama; the magistrate's drowned hamlet) reveal as one rotten root: the same rice-quota machine.
- **Act 4 — The Ledger and Your Own Name (climax, the case).** Not a duel but a case against power. He
  tracks threads down-valley to the peddler, the post-town, and the living, grown Tama (now **Oharu**),
  who fears the village that almost sold her; he confirms he is **Ren**, the porter the guild wrote off.
  With Kiku and allies he assembles mundane proof (doctored ledger, tally-stick, rigged masu, the
  guilt-sick clerk's testimony, the priest's register, a porter-witness). The powerful want the valley to
  stay "cursed" and now hunt him as a witness; the estate's complicity is threatened. He still can't win
  by force (proven again in a losing skirmish with the enforcer) — only by getting the truth *above* the
  guilty via formal petition (**osso**) to a touring inspector/the domain. Reachable culprits answer; the
  untouchable magistrate largely escapes; Kuzuhara's dead are named and shrined; Tama is freed to choose.
  He learns his family did not survive — there was never anything heroic to go back to.
- **Act 5 — Spring Again (diegetic reset / new cycle).** A grounded season-cycle time-skip, not
  reincarnation. The valley is safer but unguarded and scarred; Jūbei is too old to drill the farm boys.
  Ren — now a known, capable young man — is given standing: he accepts stewardship of the estate's fields
  and trails, *or* sets out with Kiku (and Genza, and Oharu if she chooses) to re-found Kuzuhara as a
  tiny resettlement and open a humble training-and-workhouse for other famine orphans and strays. The
  calendar turns to spring; the cramped estate-tenant UI and early caps are left behind, the map and
  economy expand a tier, and **all** hard-won skill, reputation, recipes, tools, koku, and relationships
  carry forward as meta-progress — and now as the baseline he **teaches** to NPC students (idle
  producers/fighters). Effort-over-talent becomes literally generational: a man who climbed once now
  building the ladder for others, starting them from exactly the zero he started at. New, farther-off
  kamikakushi rumors drift up from distant valleys, seeding the next cycle on the same grounded promise.

## Key NPCs (each pulls double duty: a yokai-debunk *and* a mystery clue, by design)

- **Jūbei** — aging lower-samurai retainer / lone estate guardsman; competent-but-never-great old
  foot-soldier with bad knees. Mentor & combat/training gatekeeper; embodiment of the no-talent/all-effort
  theme; speaks the creed. Carries famine-era guilt (his estate's fields held while Kuzuhara drowned).
  His decline motivates founding the training hall at the reset.
- **Sayo** — the steward's daughter (~16), sharp, kind, keeper of the valley's informal news. Guide,
  emotional anchor, and the living heart of the red herring (she names him "Tama" and needs him to be her
  lost friend). Tutorializes the farm/economy loop; gates early reputation; channels rumor-as-gameplay.
  The person the truth will most hurt and most free (her own family's complicity in the rice debt).
- **Obaa Sato** — village herbalist/wise-woman who dresses his head wound. Grounding voice & lore-keeper;
  names his symptoms (concussion, fever) rather than visions; narrates folk-belief sympathetically while
  knowing it's coping; dispenses early survival/foraging/crafting knowledge.
- **Kiku** — a survivor living in the ruins of the drowned upstream valley. The "vengeful spirit"
  resolved into a person — a fellow Kuzuhara child who holds the memory Ren lost. Mystery-key,
  co-investigator, found-family anchor; co-founds the resettlement at the reset. *(Portray with dignity —
  a traumatized disaster survivor, not a "wild person" trope.)*
- **Genza (the "tengu")** — a fugitive ex-foot-soldier living rough in the high cedars. Grounded-yokai
  reveal, mountain-craft teacher, and key witness in one. A second, gentler model of the effort theme.
- **Kuroiwa** — the deputy magistrate's agent (daikan's man); outwardly lawful, polite, patient,
  dangerous. Primary human antagonist & local architect of the tax-quota cover-up and smuggling racket;
  benefits from the comforting myth; hunts Ren as a witness. Defeated by evidence, not violence — and the
  magistrate above him largely escapes (keeping the resolution honest about power).
- **Headman Yagōemon** — the village headman (shōya) who doctored the ledgers and let the "cursed
  village" story reach the magistrate. Not a monster — a frightened middle-man who chose his own neck.
  The humanly-reachable culprit.
- **Hanzaki** — a masterless swordsman enforcer, scarred over one eye (the "one-eyed mountain god").
  Recurring physical threat and **thematic mirror**: real talent gone rotten vs. Ren's earned competence.
  Out-fights Ren every early encounter; the eventual confrontation is survived by trained endurance, not
  out-talenting him. *(See refinement: show his edge as partly trained, not pure innate gift.)*
- **Clerk Naozane** — a guilt-sick junior clerk who transcribed the lies. Mystery-key & witness; the
  crack in the cover-up; supplies documentary proof that makes the osso petition possible.
- **Oharu (the real Tama)** — the "spirited-away" child, alive and grown in a down-valley post-town,
  terrified of the home that almost sold her. Mystery payoff and living proof; the reunion is deliberately
  costly and incomplete (she may not forgive; some adults are never absolved).
- **The peddler Sōkichi** — itinerant medicine-seller. Quest-giver & information broker; the engine that
  lets the player chase clues beyond the home village; a grounded source of yokai tales he can explain.

## Emotional hook

The ache of being handed an identity you didn't earn and can't be sure you want — and the harder dignity
of becoming someone real anyway, by your own hands. A whole grieving valley *needs* him to be their lost
child; the kindest people (Sayo above all) will be hurt by the truth. The player feels two pulls: the
longing to simply *be* the miracle everyone wants and slip into a ready-made home, and the truer dignity
of refusing the borrowed name and building a real self callus by callus. The grind loop makes that
dignity tangible — every number climbing is proof he's constructing an honest self rather than wearing a
dead child's. The catharsis is never power: a nobody teenager becomes the person who lets a grieving
sister mourn honestly, reunites a frightened woman with the home that wronged her, and makes a forgotten
village's name be spoken aloud. The hours of grind *are* the character's hard-won belonging.

## How the story paces the incremental unlocks

Story access and UI unlocks are the **same currency** — trust and competence, both bought with labor — so
the incremental reveal is fully diegetic and numbers-go-up advances in lockstep with truth-coming-out:

- Act 0 keeps the screen near-empty (body/rest bar, rice counter, one/two tasks) — recovery *is* the
  tutorial, justifying minimal UI.
- Surviving the first harvest unlocks the peaceful economy panel by panel (farm → forage → woodcut →
  fish → craft → koku), each tied to rising stamina and NPCs granting access.
- The humbling first defeat is the gate that unlocks the entire training & idle-combat suite — the player
  *wants* the new buttons because the story just proved he needs them; it stays grind-gated so the climb
  is felt.
- Rising reputation (earned by sustained labor & survived seasons) unlocks NPC confessions, expedition
  routes, and craft tiers — mystery progress is literally purchased with grind.
- Investigation gates exploration: each debunked folk-belief opens a new, more dangerous area (the ford →
  mountains & forbidden ruins → the pass), unlocking deeper gathering, expedition logistics, tougher idle
  combat.
- The conspiracy gates an evidence/influence layer (gathering proof, swaying witnesses, the petition).
- Small story fragments drip on a steady cadence (sensory flashes, peddler rumors, dream-name beats) tied
  to concrete thresholds so curiosity never goes cold.
- The Act 5 time-skip re-gates a whole new valley-tier of systems plus a teaching/management layer behind
  the veteran's standing — restarting the unlock cadence at a higher floor.

## Adversarial verification — verdict: **PASS**

> The spine honors all six hard constraints with unusual discipline: mundane amnesia; a genuinely &
> mechanically mediocre protagonist with no hidden edge; every yokai resolving one-to-one to a
> human/natural cause; no isekai/reincarnation/time-loop; a rural goshi estate with kamikakushi as
> believed folklore covering a human truth; a grounded season-cycle/founding-a-school reset carrying
> hard-won competence forward. **No outright violations.**

### Refinements to apply (from the verifier — fold into the PRD)

1. **Ambiguity budget.** Pick exactly **one** residual-ambiguity token to ever leave unresolved per
   playthrough (recommend the unidentified-hand offering — most mundane-readable). Give the fox and the
   shrine-omen quiet optional mundane explanations the player can discover.
2. **Re-ground or cut the guiding fox** (the most genre-coded "kitsune-made-real" beat). Rewrite its two
   "rescues" as transparently self-interested animal behavior (scavenging trail food / denning by the
   only dry ledge / bolting from the same boar) so it reads as coincidence, not aid.
3. **The half-drowning dream is reconstructed sensory memory** — a real voice he heard in the water
   (Kiku's), recalled — *not* precognition or a psychic link. Make this explicit so it can't read as a
   supernatural tether.
4. **Lock returning-memory beats as flavor-only at the systems level.** Add an implementation guardrail:
   NO returning memory or instinctive habit ever grants a starting stat, recipe, or combat bonus — every
   number starts at the bottom and is earned.
5. **Tune Hanzaki** so his superiority is veteran skill + brutality + better tools/conditioning, not pure
   innate gift (let one line acknowledge he too "drilled for years") — the talent-mirror must never prove
   innate talent decides fights in this world.
6. **Specify the drip cadence concretely** — a max number of in-game days / a koku-or-skill threshold
   between guaranteed story fragments, plus at least one always-available, non-reputation-gated
   investigation thread, so mystery progress never fully stalls behind grind.
7. **Reset branches.** Designate one branch canonical-default (recommend re-founding Kuzuhara — best pays
   off "name the drowned / build the ladder"); frame estate-stewardship as an equally valid alternative
   with identical meta-progress carry-over, so neither reads as the lesser ending.
8. **Keep the darkest beats off-screen and reported** (testimony, ledgers, a survivor's account) rather
   than depicted, and protect the cozy daily loop as the dominant minute-to-minute texture so grim
   revelations land as weight, not as the game's mood.

### Authenticity notes (Edo-period accuracy — research before authoring §5)

- **goshi** (郷士) holding a small estate, working its own fields, extending rice debt to tenants is
  plausible (status/wealth varied by domain; keep the estate modest, not lordly — a single aging retainer
  is apt).
- **daikan/tedai** district administration running a tax-quota cover-up; falsified village ledgers
  (shōya doctoring records) — well-attested abuses. Sound.
- **osso** (越訴) — direct over-the-head peasant petition — was real and *genuinely dangerous*;
  petitioners risked execution even when right. **The most authenticity-sensitive mechanic** — portray
  the danger honestly (punished even when vindicated), not as a clean legal win.
- **corvée** (kuniyaku) labor & **seki** (堰, weir/embankment) upkeep diverted from a poor upstream
  hamlet to protect tax fields — plausible, resonant with real Edo flood-control politics.
- **masu** (枡) measuring-box fraud as evidence — authentic (the shogunate's kyō-masu standard, and
  cheating on it, were real points of conflict).
- **kamikakushi** as a folk explanation for vanished children/travelers — well-documented; treat
  respectfully as grief-coping & cover, not mockery.
- **Sensitivity:** Kiku & the yamanba-widow are *traumatized survivors of disaster/poverty with dignity
  and agency*, never "wild people to be tamed." (The "mercy quest, not a kill" framing is good — extend
  to Kiku.)
- Bon offerings, jizō, shimenawa, soul-calling rites, a priest's register of vanished travelers — all
  period-plausible folk-religious texture; keep ritual detail specific & respectful, not generic "Shinto."
- Indentured porter working off a parent's debt; kaidō porter/caravan culture; smuggled silver on
  tax-rice routes — plausible. Inter-domain movement was checkpoint-controlled (**sekisho**), so flood
  displacement (not free travel) is the *right* authentic mechanism for crossing into the valley.
- Edo speech was sharply **status-marked** — status-appropriate address (goshi household vs. tenants vs.
  daikan's agent) is a cheap authenticity win.
- Famine + relief-rice diversion + drowned poorest hamlet echoes real Tenmei/Tenpō episodes — keep it
  **generic-rural**, not pinned to a real named disaster/place.

## Top risks (carry into the PRD)

- Mystery could read as a stock "corrupt official runs smuggling" plot — freshness must come from the
  *specific* human textures (mistaken-identity legend, gender/age drift as fair clues, rigged masu &
  tally-stick, the estate's own complicity, the partial/bitter justice), not the premise.
- The "you were never Tama" / "just a porter" reveal can feel like a cheat — plant fair, re-readable clues
  early so it's earned; make the chosen-name resolution satisfying regardless.
- Tonal whiplash between cozy labor and grim material — modulate deliberately; off-screen restraint;
  found-family counterweight.
- No-magic-in-a-folklore-setting → Scooby-Doo risk — enforce the 1:1 belief→cause table; debunk with
  dawning dread, not gotchas; keep residual ambiguity rare & mundane-readable.
- Honoring the mediocre start fights the genre's fast-dopamine instinct — the humbling must motivate, not
  demoralize; the peaceful economy must be generously rewarding within each act.
- Mystery-vs-grind pacing; partial-justice landing; antagonist nuance (no mustache-twirlers); reunion
  kept costly; cast double-duty to avoid bloat; the reset framed as a reward, not a punishment.
