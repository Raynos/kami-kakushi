## rung R4 · rung-r4
speaker: kihei
voice: arms
motivates: tab-combat, panel-drill-yard, panel-bestiary

> Six days since the wolf. The sickroom to the board is forty paces; he
> takes them the way Sōan showed him — hand on the rail, breath kept
> shallow, the ribs held like a load badly tied.

> Past the kitchen passage a screen stands open on a corridor he has never
> been sent down. An altar set into the passage wall; rites laid out for
> somebody; a pair of straw sandals, new this year, facing away from the
> house. He looks a breath too long. The screen slides shut from inside.
> Nobody says anything to him about it, then or after.

> The kitchen has told the wolf story three times since morning, and it
> has grown at each telling. In the yard's version he held the beast down
> and throttled it against the sill. Nobody asks the man limping past what
> happened. They have the better wolf already.

> Genemon is at the board, grinding ink.

Nameless (player): "The book should have it right."

Nameless (player): "The night-door bar is broken. The lamp is broken. The
watch went unmanned while I was down. The wolf is alive — it went out
bleeding, on its own legs. I didn't drive it. I was underneath it when it
chose to go."

> Genemon writes while he talks. It is the only part of the steward that
> ever hurries.

Genemon: "A bar. A lamp — forty mon the pair. Three nights of watch made
good by Kihei. Six days of a hand at half work — say eight."

Genemon: "The wolf. Dead or gone?"

Nameless (player): "Gone."

Genemon: "Then I enter it open."

> He blots the page and reaches for the next matter. The man at the board
> waits a breath for either blame or thanks, and learns that the book pays
> out neither.

> The tale does not consult the book. By evening the kitchen's wolf is
> twice the size of the day-book's. The day-book's is the one with his
> blood on the sill. He sleeps badly on the difference, and at grey light
> he crosses to the drill yard.

> Kihei is at the rack, going down the practice staves with an oiled rag,
> one by one, the way a man does a job that is his alone. Stalls for
> twenty horses stand empty behind him. The house owns one mule.

> On the wall above the stalls the heir's boy is watching. He is always
> watching. Nobody sends him away, and nobody calls him down.

Nameless (player): "Will you teach me?"

> Kihei goes on down the rack. One stave. Another. A man who wants an
> answer from this yard can stand in it and want one.

Nameless (player): "Teach me. I'm asking."

> The second time costs more than the walk over did.

Kihei: "…Lift the left arm. Stop where it stops."

> He is looked over the way Kihei goes over a girth strap before trusting
> a load to it — without hope, and without hurry.

Kihei: "Cracked, not broken. You will fall badly for a season."

Kihei: "I have dug graves on this ground for men who could not tell a
mamushi from a rat-snake. I do not intend to dig the next one."

Kihei: "Talent is a story the lucky tell. You are not lucky. So you will
work."

### ask r4-first · "What do I learn first?"

Kihei: "Falling. You did it badly at the kura."

### ask r4-wolf · "Will the wolf come back?"

Kihei: "Bled like that, it dens up and mends. By midwinter it remembers
where the rice was. They always remember."

### ask r4-stalls · "There are stalls for twenty."

> Kihei folds the rag once and starts on the next stave. The question is
> left standing in the yard with the man who asked it.
